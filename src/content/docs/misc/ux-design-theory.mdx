UX (User Experience) design focuses on understanding users and creating products that provide meaningful, relevant experiences. This guide covers the fundamental principles of UX design.

---

## What is UX Design?

UX design is about genuinely understanding users of an application. Every click, scroll, page refresh, and survey data point is collected and analyzed to understand their:

- **Wishes** - What they want
- **Needs** - What they require
- **Desires** - What they aspire to
- **Blockers** - What frustrates them

The goal is to improve the overall customer experience.

---

## The UX Process

### Step 1: Understand User Needs

Find or understand user needs through surveys and data collection tools.

### Step 2: Define Problems

From these needs, identify problems and challenges users face.

### Step 3: Find Solutions

Develop solutions through trial and error. Be willing to iterate and go back to previous steps.

<Aside type="tip" title="Iteration is Key">
  UX design is not linear. You may need to revisit earlier steps as you learn
  more about your users.
</Aside>

## Cognitive Psychology in UX

Cognitive psychology studies the mental mechanisms behind human behavior.

### Perception

Perception changes based on:

- Current state (tired, focused, rushed)
- Environment (mobile, desktop, noisy)
- Objectives (browsing, purchasing, researching)

### Language

Adapt communication to match how users speak and think.

### Memory & Attention

Minimize cognitive load for actions on your interface. Users can only hold 7¬±2 items in short-term memory.

### Problem Solving

Guide users at every interaction point to prevent frustration.

## Psychological Laws in UX

### Reciprocity

Users like to give back when they receive value. Promotions feel like gifts and encourage engagement.

### Social Proof

Users trust products used and appreciated by many others. Use testimonials, ratings, and reviews.

### Scarcity

Items perceived as rare are more desirable. Limited-time offers create urgency.

### Affordance

Visual characteristics suggest how an object can be used. A button should look clickable.

### Fitts's Law

The time to reach a target is proportional to distance and inversely proportional to target size.

> **Takeaway:** Make important buttons large and easily accessible.

### Hick's Law & Miller's Law

- Humans struggle to retain information (7¬±2 items)
- Keep navigation to 7¬±2 items
- Offer binary choices when possible to reduce user effort

## User Personas

A persona is an aggregate of similar users who will use your application.

Create personas after conducting thorough user research to identify user groups with similar:

- Demographics
- Goals
- Behaviors
- Pain points

**Format:** Create a profile card like a CV with photo, name, bio, goals, and frustrations.

---

## Information Architecture

The goal is to organize information so users can easily find what they're looking for.

Use personas to guide your architecture decisions. Ask: "How would this persona navigate to find X?"

## Benchmarking

Benchmarking identifies best practices by studying how others solve similar problems.

### Types of Benchmarking

| Type            | Description                                              |
| --------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Internal**    | Compare operations within your organization              |
| **Functional**  | Compare similar functions in non-competing companies     |
| **Competitive** | Compare against direct competitors                       |
| **Generic**     | Compare practices across completely different industries |

### Benchmarking Process

1. Explore competitor interfaces
2. Analyze, compare, identify strengths and weaknesses
3. Document findings (UX and functional)

## Rule 1: Organize Your Pages Well

- Perfect your navigation
- Organize and rhythm content
- Request information intelligently
- Focus on essential content and features
- Remove unnecessary interactions
- Respect typography and color standards
- Practice visual hierarchy
- Reduce the number of CTAs (Call-to-Action)

---

## Rule 2: Leverage Modern Web/Mobile Patterns

- Capitalize on conventions and standards
- Consider user's country of origin
- Help users accomplish tasks faster
- Limit visible form fields
- Save form entries for returning users

---

## Rule 3: Always Inform the User

- Identify who you are
- Show where the user is in the site
- Provide feedback on what will happen next
- Inform about availability
- Convince and reassure
- Optimize purchase funnels
- Respect affordance principles
- Choose appropriate terms and icons

---

## Rule 4: Anticipate Errors

- Protect users from possible errors
- Help users overcome errors gracefully
- Mark required fields clearly
- Label form fields properly
- Size fields appropriately for content
- Choose the right input elements

---

## Rule 5: Give Users Control

- Don't be intrusive or impose anything
- Be flexible while maintaining conventions
- Let users undo actions
- Provide clear exit points

---

## Rule 6: Accessibility for Everyone

- Include all users regardless of ability
- Ensure proper contrast, colors, and font sizes
- Polish aesthetics
- Subtitle videos
- Account for information overload
- Ensure technical reliability
- Add social features and micro-interactions

<Aside type="caution" title="Accessibility is Not Optional">
  Designing for accessibility benefits all users, not just those with
  disabilities.
</Aside>
